# Label taxonomy: definitions and 2 examples per label
1. ## Hype & Support

Definition: Tweets cheering for a team or player, expressing fandom, excitement, or admiration — positive emotion with no substantive criticism or analysis.

Examples:

"ALL HAIL THE KING! Absolutely amazing! #WhateverItTakes"
"Gooooo GS Warriors!!! #StephenCurry"
"Thank you @KingJames for being who you are."

Uncertain case:

"HOW YOU JUST GON LET @KingJames OOP ON YOU LIKE THAT?!?!! GOOD GOD!!!"

(Is this hype for LeBron, or mockery of the defender? Excitement and trash talk can overlap.)

2. ## Criticism & Trash Talk

Definition: Tweets mocking, insulting, or expressing negative judgment about a player or team — including frustration with one's own team.

Examples:

"Draymond Sucksssss"
"What's Kevin Durant's nickname again? Soft Baby Bad-actor"
"I can't stand watching the Cavs' inexplicably lazy defense down 0-2."

Uncertain case:

"Reduce the swag, FOCUS Warriors!!!"

(Frustrated criticism, or supportive coaching from a fan? Negative tone but rooting interest.)

3. ## Game/Tactical Analysis

Definition: Tweets making a substantive observation about strategy, matchups, momentum, or what a team should do — reasoning about the game, not just feeling about it.

Examples:

"Double teaming Steph, minimum fouls, minimum turnovers and connecting from 3PT range can ensure a win."
"Isn't it amazing how much better a team can look at home compared to on the road?"
"No lead is safe, so the Cavs better protect that margin before the 3rd quarter."

Uncertain case:

"Finally Kevin Love is BULLYING out there."

(Is this an analytical read on Love's play, or just excitement? Light analysis vs. hype.)

4. ## Officiating Complaint

Definition: Tweets disputing referee decisions, non-calls, or alleging unfair/biased officiating.

Examples:

"Bad no call refs!! 10:44 2nd Qtr under the Cavs basket!!!"
"Didn't know a body check was a legal screen."
"@OfficialNBARefs Really! You gentlemen have no making up to do."

Uncertain case:

"That's a travel in FIBA."

(A rules observation that could be neutral analysis, or an implied complaint that the ref missed it.)


# Data collection source, labeling process, label distribution, and 3 difficult-to-label examples with your decisions
## Data Collection and Labeling

### Data Source

Tweets were collected from discussions on X (formerly Twitter) during **Game 3 of the 2018 NBA Finals between the Cleveland Cavaliers and Golden State Warriors**. This community was chosen because live sports conversations contain a wide range of discourse quality, from emotional reactions and memes to thoughtful analysis of players, coaching decisions, and officiating.

### Labeling Process

The initial labels were generated automatically using the **Llama-3.3-70B** model through `prelabel_groq.py`. Each tweet was assigned a discourse-quality label based on the amount of reasoning and evidence present in the text.

However, the same model family was later used as the zero-shot baseline. Consequently, the baseline was effectively evaluated against labels generated by itself, creating circularity in the evaluation. The resulting score (~0.77) therefore measures agreement with its own outputs rather than providing an independent estimate of classification quality.

Meanwhile, the DistilBERT classifier had to learn from 151 automatically labeled tweets. Since these labels were never human-reviewed, the model was learning from noisy supervision and consistently plateaued around 0.47 accuracy regardless of the number of training epochs.

Thus, the difference in performance should not be interpreted as evidence that zero-shot LLMs are inherently superior to fine-tuning. Instead, it reflects the importance of having reliable human-annotated ground truth.

### Label Distribution

The dataset consists of 151 tweets distributed across discourse-quality classes. Because the labels were generated automatically, the distribution reflects the predictions of Llama-3.3-70B rather than manually curated annotations.

# Fine-tuning approach: base model, training setup, and at least one hyperparameter decision
## Fine-Tuning Approach

### Base Model

The classification model was built using **DistilBERT**, a lightweight transformer model derived from BERT. DistilBERT was selected because it offers strong language understanding while requiring significantly fewer parameters and computational resources than the original BERT model.

### Training Setup

The model was fine-tuned on 151 tweets collected from discussions surrounding **Game 3 of the 2018 NBA Finals between the Cleveland Cavaliers and Golden State Warriors**. Tweets were tokenized using the DistilBERT tokenizer and used to train a supervised classifier that predicts discourse quality.

Training was performed for multiple epochs while monitoring validation performance. Despite experimenting with different epoch values, validation accuracy consistently plateaued around 0.47, suggesting that dataset size and label quality were larger limiting factors than training duration.

### Hyperparameter Decisions

#### Number of Epochs

Several epoch values were explored. Increasing the number of epochs did not lead to substantial improvements and risked overfitting the small dataset, so a moderate number of epochs was chosen.

#### Learning Rate

A relatively small learning rate was used to allow the pretrained DistilBERT weights to adapt gradually to the classification task while preserving the linguistic knowledge acquired during pretraining.

#### Batch Size

A small batch size was selected because of hardware constraints and the limited size of the dataset. Smaller batches also introduce some stochasticity that can help regularization.

### Limitations

The primary limitation of the fine-tuning process was the dataset itself. The training labels were generated automatically using Llama-3.3-70B and were not initially human-reviewed. Consequently, DistilBERT was learning from noisy labels. Since the zero-shot baseline used the same model family that generated the labels, the comparison between the two approaches should be interpreted cautiously.


# Baseline description: prompt used and how results were collected
## Zero-Shot Baseline

### Baseline Model

A zero-shot baseline was established using **Llama-3.3-70B**, which performs classification without additional training. The model relies on its pretrained knowledge and a prompt describing the task.

### Prompt Used

```text
You are evaluating the quality of discourse in NBA-related tweets posted during the 2018 NBA Finals Game 3 between the Cleveland Cavaliers and Golden State Warriors.

Classify the following tweet according to the strength of reasoning and evidence presented.

Tweet:
"{tweet}"

Return only the label.
```

Each tweet in the test set was processed independently.

### How Results Were Collected

Predictions were generated by passing every tweet to Llama-3.3-70B and recording the returned label. These predictions were then compared with the labels in the test set to compute evaluation metrics.

The zero-shot baseline achieved an accuracy of approximately 0.77. However, this result should be interpreted cautiously because the test labels were originally generated using the same model family through `prelabel_groq.py`. Consequently, the baseline was effectively being evaluated against labels produced by itself, creating circularity in the evaluation.

As a result, the reported score largely measures agreement with the model's own earlier predictions rather than providing an independent measure of discourse-classification quality. A more reliable comparison would require human-reviewed labels so that both the zero-shot baseline and the fine-tuned DistilBERT model are evaluated against an independent ground truth.

# Reflection: what the model learned vs. what you intended
# Reflection

## What the Model Learned vs. What I Intended

The goal of this project was to build a classifier that distinguishes between high-quality and low-quality discourse in tweets posted by NBA fans reacting to **Game 3 of the 2018 NBA Finals between the Cleveland Cavaliers and Golden State Warriors**. Ideally, high-quality discourse would consist of tweets containing reasoning, evidence, or basketball analysis, while low-quality discourse would include emotional reactions, insults, memes, and unsupported claims.

However, the model learned something slightly different from what I originally intended.

Because the dataset consisted of only 151 tweets and the labels were generated automatically using Llama-3.3-70B without human review, the fine-tuned DistilBERT model often appeared to rely on superficial patterns rather than truly understanding discourse quality. For example, shorter and more emotional tweets were frequently associated with low-quality discourse, while longer tweets containing basketball terminology were more likely to be classified as high quality.

The evaluation also revealed an important limitation in the experimental setup. The zero-shot baseline used the same model family that generated the original labels, meaning that its accuracy of approximately 0.77 was inflated by circularity. In contrast, DistilBERT had to learn from noisy labels and consistently plateaued around 0.47 accuracy. Therefore, the performance difference does not necessarily indicate that zero-shot prompting is inherently superior to fine-tuning.

One of the most important lessons from this project is that **data quality matters more than model complexity**. Even a powerful transformer model cannot overcome inconsistent or noisy supervision. A relatively small amount of carefully reviewed human annotations would likely improve performance more than simply increasing the number of training epochs or changing hyperparameters.

Another lesson is that evaluation metrics alone can be misleading. A higher accuracy score does not always imply a better model if the benchmark itself is biased. Understanding how labels are created and ensuring independence between training, testing, and baseline methods are essential for making meaningful comparisons.

If I were to continue this project, I would first perform human review of all labels, expand the dataset with additional NBA discussions, and establish a truly independent test set. I would also explore more nuanced categories of discourse quality and compare fine-tuned models against zero-shot methods using manually verified ground truth. These improvements would provide a more reliable assessment of what the models are actually learning and how effectively they capture the quality of online sports discourse.


# Spec reflection: one way the spec helped you, one way implementation diverged from it and why
# Specification Reflection

## How the Specification Helped

Creating a specification before implementation helped clarify the objective of the project. The original goal was to classify the quality of discourse in NBA fans' reactions during **Game 3 of the 2018 NBA Finals between the Cleveland Cavaliers and Golden State Warriors**. Defining the classes and evaluation criteria in advance provided a clear framework for data collection, model selection, and performance assessment. The specification also encouraged thinking about discourse quality in terms of reasoning and evidence rather than simply sentiment, which made the task more meaningful.

## How the Implementation Diverged from the Specification

One important way the implementation diverged from the original specification was in the labeling process. The intended workflow assumed that the dataset would contain reliable ground-truth labels reflecting the distinction between high-quality and low-quality discourse. In practice, the labels were generated automatically using Llama-3.3-70B and were not initially reviewed by humans. This decision made it possible to create a dataset quickly, but it introduced label noise and affected the validity of the evaluation.

Another consequence of this choice was that the zero-shot baseline used the same model family that generated the labels. As a result, the baseline was effectively evaluated against its own outputs, producing an inflated accuracy of approximately 0.77. Meanwhile, the fine-tuned DistilBERT model had to learn from noisy labels and plateaued around 0.47 accuracy.

This divergence occurred primarily because of time constraints and the desire to rapidly create a labeled dataset. In retrospect, a smaller dataset with carefully reviewed human annotations would have been more consistent with the original specification and would have produced a more reliable comparison between the zero-shot and fine-tuned approaches.

Overall, the project reinforced the importance of treating the specification as a guide rather than a rigid blueprint. While the implementation successfully produced a working classifier, it also revealed how choices made during data collection and labeling can significantly influence the conclusions drawn from model performance.
